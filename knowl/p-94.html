We noted above that a linear function has the form \(f(x)=mx+b\text{,}\) where \(m\) is the slope of the line, and \(b\) is the \(y\)-intercept. Since \(m\) and \(b\) can take on various values, taken together, they represent a family of functions. For example, we could fix \(b = 2\text{,}\) and then draw the graphs of \(f(x)=mx+2\) for various values of \(m\text{;}\) for example, \(m = -1, -2, 2, 1\text{.}\) Doing so would give the functions in the family \(f(x)=mx+2\) shown in the left image of FigureÂ 16.
